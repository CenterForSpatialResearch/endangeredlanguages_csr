Beyond the Enclave

New York City is one of the most linguistically diverse places in the world; estimates show anywhere from 200-800 different languages represented across the five boroughs. Nowhere is this more apparent than in Queens, where many of these languages are spoken in schools, restaurants, community centers, and homes across the area.

We often think of language in a city the size of New York as centered around “ethnic enclaves,” neighborhoods where minority language speakers tend to cluster. While that model may contain some degree of truth, it obscures how deeply integrated multilingualism is into the fabric of New York City. Through these models, we invite you to explore some of the ways language is organized in daily life. Both in the home, and on the street, the linguistic diversity of New York City is more than a population count, and tangibly affects city life. Though the models are based on real places and events, the details have been altered to protect individual’s privacy.

Multilingual (UN) Building

Many buildings in New York City can be thought of as a type of miniature United Nations, where speakers of a multitude of different and unrelated languages live next door to each other. In this building, some apartments are connected through family relationships where one family moved in and then relatives began to follow (i.e., Polish, Patois, and Russian speakers). But many are not, instead connected only through the walls they share and the lingua franca they use to bridge the communicative and cultural divide.

Street Festival

Almost every weekend in the summer, a street festival can be found in the city. Though many of these events are centered around one ethnographic-linguistic group or geographic region, they take on a multilingual identity through the attendees. Individuals bring their languages with them to every interaction, sometimes in the lingua franca, sometimes in a language they speak at home, sometimes in yet another language, but always as members of multilingual society.

Food Trucks

Across the globe, food is deeply connected to language, and in diaspora communities, culinary traditions often outlast linguistic ones. Yet mealtimes often provide an opportunity for speakers to meaningfully use a language. In this setting, we see that though workers may not always speak the language associated with the food they are serving, consumers don’t always speak it either. Though individuals bring their languages with them everywhere, an onlooker may never know without talking to them.

Bilingual Building

Though this building on its own would never register as an enclave in the Census, for the residents, it is an oasis. In a quickly changing and gentrifying city, buildings such as this one form micro-communities that have the power to sustain familial, linguistic, and social ties. Most of the Polish speaking residents are related by blood, marriage, or friendship, and all have at least one connection to family or friends in Poland.
